## Research Overview
Self-folding offers a practical route to deployable architected materials: structures can be **fabricated flat** (simple printing, compact storage/transport) and then **thermally deployed** into complex 3D load-bearing forms. In this work, we designed a **cage-shaped self-folding mechanical metamaterial** that transitions from a 2D sheet into **multiple stable 3D configurations**.

The key capability is **multi-stage deployment**: the same unit cell sequentially folds into distinct modes (Mode 1 → Mode 2) when heated to different temperature thresholds, and each configuration remains stable upon cooling. This is achieved by integrating self-folding elements with **different thermal activation thresholds**, enabling staged folding within a single architecture.

---

## Why It Matters
Most self-folding metamaterials are engineered to reach only **one deployed shape**, which restricts their mechanical tunability and limits real-world adaptability. By enabling **multiple stable deployed modes** from the same initial structure, we unlock a pathway to:
- **Reconfigurable mechanical performance** (mode-dependent force–displacement response)
- **Multi-stage load-bearing** behavior (staged engagement instead of a single response regime)
- **Design decoupling**, where the mechanical contributions of different structural levels can be tuned independently and then combined

---

## Key Contributions
### 1) Multiple-step, temperature-programmable self-folding
Unlike typical systems that fold into one final geometry, our metamaterial can be thermally programmed to deploy into **two stable cage configurations**:
- **Mode 1** at ~**65°C**
- **Mode 2** at ~**85°C**

This staged sequence provides a controllable way to switch geometry—and therefore mechanical response—without redesigning the unit cell.

### 2) Tunable bistability via thermal softening
We exploit temperature as a “mechanical switch” to control when bistable elements release stored elastic energy and drive folding. This converts thermal inputs into reliable, repeatable structural state changes.

### 3) Cage geometry that enables decoupled mechanical design
The cage architecture supports **multi-stage load-bearing** and makes performance more designable: multi-level cages can be interpreted as a **parallel combination of nonlinear spring-like contributions**, which enables decoupling—design each level’s response, then superimpose them.

### 4) Predictive mechanics model + FEM-to-experiment validation
Beyond demonstrating the concept, we developed a simplified but accurate **mechanical model** (supported by FEM and experiments) to quantitatively predict nonlinear compressive responses across modes. This turns the work from “cool folding” into an engineering design methodology.

## Methodology (End-to-End Research Pipeline)
This project was executed as a full research loop rather than a single isolated experiment:

1. **Concept & architecture design** (unit cell → single-level and dual-level cages)  
2. **Mechanism engineering** (bistable elements + elastomeric film actuation; threshold design)  
3. **Numerical simulation** (FEM-driven iteration to de-risk geometry and response)  
4. **Mechanical analysis** (reduced-order modeling to interpret and predict nonlinear compression)  
5. **Additive manufacturing & assembly** (multi-material fabrication and repeatable builds)  
6. **Experimental verification** (deployment demonstrations + compression tests)  
7. **Model fitting & validation** (closing the loop between theory, simulation, and measurements)  
8. **Manuscript drafting & revision** (figures, writing, peer review cycles)
